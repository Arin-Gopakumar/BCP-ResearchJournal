Evaluating the Evolving Determinants of Environmental Concern in the United States: A Decade of Change

Ryan Zerbib '25

Climate change poses an existential threat to humanity, yet implementing effective policies to mitigate its impacts has proven challenging due to the need for widespread public support. Environmental concern (EC) among the general populace is a crucial factor influencing climate policy adoption. This study evaluates the relative determinants of EC in the United States and how those factors have evolved over the past decade by analyzing data from the 2020 International Social Survey Programme using linear regression analysis. Building upon prior research utilizing the same data source from 2010, this investigation offers updated insights into demographic characteristics, values/trust indicators, and economic factors underlying EC levels. Key findings include gender, age, governmental trust, and relative income as significant predictors of EC, with women, younger individuals, those with higher trust in government, and higher-income groups exhibiting heightened concern.

The Influence of Infinite Scroll Video Content on Cognitive Time

Dean Ketcham '25

This research demonstrates that consumers underestimate the amount of time that has elapsed while scrolling on social media vs. watching a TV show, and is the first to demonstrate a causal relationship between the infinite scrolling on social media and cognitive time. Importantly, despite the fact that consumers do indeed lose track of time while scrolling on social media, it shows that consumers perceive time to have passed at a normal rate, meaning that they are unaware of any changes in cognitive time (an individual's subjective perception of time).

Social Media and the Portrayal of the Male Diet in Fitness

Marco Pannozzo '25

This study aims to describe how the male diet is portrayed within the fitness industry on the social media platform TikTok. I used qualitative content analysis of selected popular TikTok videos on the topic of bodybuilding, defined as the effort to become more lean and toned. Bodybuilding is the most prominent form of male fitness content on social media and has been extensively studied. The way that social media use interacts with body image and fitness efforts is dynamic and a prevalent area of future research. The current body of research has established a link between social media use and body image concerns that lead to disordered or altered eating habits, however, the content itself surrounding bodybuilding on social media has not been analyzed. This study aims to fill that gap and broaden the understanding of how social media influences how males eat.

Religiosity and its Effects on Fantasy Literature

Santiago Paul '25

Religion and reading play important roles in the daily life of any given individual, influencing their behavior, community, and worldview. However, the relationship between how these two factors influence the other remains understudied, as while it is known the religion can influence behavior, which can in turn influence reading interests, studies have merely looked at the two in isolation, and never together. Similarly, many have drawn conclusions that the values present within fantasy literature are “at odds” with that of many religions, but no study has drawn conclusions from lived individual experiences, but rather based solely on moral conjectures. Thus, the goal of this research is to identify whether religiosity and reading interest, specifically regarding fantasy literature, are correlated with each other, to determine if they are indeed strong influencing factors. Utilizing a Likert model survey, responses were correlated with one another to determine if the two values were positively, or negatively correlated. Ultimately, it was determined that there was marginally significant correlation between the two, however large distinctions were discovered between the different subgroups involved; specifically surrounding age and subject interest. Thus, the paper attempts to guide future research in the direction of drawing conclusions based on the smaller, more nuanced subgroups, rather than attempting to make sweeping claims that may be unfounded.

Influence of Language on Emotional Reflection: A Study of Spanish Bilinguals

Rowdy Benear '25

This study investigated how bilingual Spanish-English speakers experience and express emotions differently across their two languages. Ten Hispanic-American male high school students participated in structured interviews about emotional experiences, conducted separately in English and Spanish. Participants also completed the Positive and Negative Affect Schedule (PANAS) questionnaire before, between, and after the interviews to assess changes in mood state. Qualitative analysis of the interview transcripts revealed three prominent themes: 1) happiness from relationships and goal attainment, 2) stress and anxiety around major life transitions, and 3) strong ties to family and cultural identity. Quantitative analysis showed that positive affect increased while negative affect decreased over the course of the interviews, with the most substantial shift occurring after the Spanish interview. Additionally, specific emotional markers like "happy" and "proud" were expressed with greater intensity and frequency in Spanish compared to English. The findings suggest that these bilingual participants experienced heightened emotional resonance, particularly for positive and culture-related emotions when using their native Spanish language. Implications for counseling approaches, supporting cultural identities, academic settings, and the benefits of bilingualism are discussed. Although mainly limited by a small sample, this study contributes insights into the complex interplay between language and emotional reflection.
